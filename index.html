Identifying Exoplanets

Before humanity sets off on a mission to a habitable exoplanet, we must first identify the presence of the exoplanet and analyse its habitability. As of 2019, 4048 exoplanets have been discovered. Of these, just 55 are deemed to be habitable according to the Planetary Habitability Lab.

New exoplanets are being discovered at an ever increasing rate...

The first known exoplanet, HD 114762 b, was discovered in 1989. It took 3 years to discover the next, but exoplanet hunting really took off in 2009 following the launch of the Kepler Space Telescope. This NASA telescope was designed specifically to find Earth-size habitable exoplanets in the Milky Way galaxy. Kepler is responsible for the discovery of 530,506 stars and 2,662 exoplanets.

Proxima Centauri b orbits the closest star to our Sun and lies within it's habitable zone. It's greatest appeal is its proximity to Earth, making future missions a very real possibility. In fact, there are already plans to send an unmanned spacecraft called Breakthrough Starshot to the planet. The proposed spacecraft would take approximately 20 years to reach Proxima Centauri b and a further 4 years to inform Earth of its arrival.

Proxima Centauri b is an exciting exoplanet but we have discovered several more exoplanets that are more Earth-like. Proxima Centauri b sits 8th in the Earth Similarity Index (ESI) League Table. The ESI measures how 'Earth-like' a planet is by considering several factors such as planet's radius, density and surface gravity. The measure is between 0 and 1, where the higher the score, the more similar the planet is to Earth. Which exoplanet is the most similar to Earth?







Teegarden's Star b is the most Earth-like planet discovered to date, according to ESI. The planet lies within its star's habitable zone meaning that it has the potential to hold liquid water on it's surface. It is also only 5% more massive than Earth and has a similar average temperature. Whats more, this planet is only 12 light years away which by cosmic standards is very nearby.

Two exoplanets orbiting the TRAPPIST-1 star also appear in the table. In fact, this star system holds four habitable planets. This makes this interstellar solar system so exciting to the scientific community. Check out this next plot to learn more about the TRAPPIST-1 planets.

Trappist-1 d is likely to be the most habitable of the planets in this solar system. It has the highest ESI at 0.89 and also has a similar average temperature to Earth. However, the issue with these planets is that they orbit so close to their sun. Due to this, any atmosphere on these planets may be stripped away by solar flares. Another issue with orbiting so close to the star is that the planets could become tidally locked. That is to say that one side of the planet will face the sun at all times, similar to how one side of the Moon always faces Earth. This will cause one side of the planet to be boiling hot, and the other side to be freezing cold. However, even if TRAPPIST-1 d is tidally locked, life as we know it could still exist in a narrow band around the centre of the planet.

Will we ever visit an exoplanet?

The desire for exploration is in the DNA of a human being. One day we will send unmanned probes, and perhaps even humans, to an exoplanet. But are interstellar journeys feasible and do we have the technology in order to do it?

Using previous technologies, Apollo 11 and Voyager 1, space travel is simply not feasible. This is far too long and goes to show both the vastness of space and the need for technological advancement.

However, a trip to Proxima Centauri seems possible when we look at future planned probes, the Parker Solar Probe and Breathrough Starshot. The latter is an unmanned probe that is designed to be able to travel to Proxima Centauri b in approximately 20 years. Much more technological improvement may be needed to make journeys beyond Proxima Centauri feasible.
